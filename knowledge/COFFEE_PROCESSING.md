# Coffee Processing Methods

How coffee cherries are turned into green beans — and why it matters for espresso extraction.

"Processing" in specialty coffee means everything that happens between picking the ripe cherry and shipping the dried green seed. Each method leaves a different chemical fingerprint on the bean, which directly affects how you should brew it.

---

## Why Processing Affects Espresso

Three mechanisms connect processing to your cup:

1. **Sugar and acid development.** Mucilage (the sticky fruit layer around the seed) is rich in sugars and organic acids. Methods that leave mucilage in contact with the seed longer allow more fermentation, producing fruity esters and lowering pH. Methods that wash it away quickly yield a cleaner, more origin-transparent cup.

2. **Bean density and porosity.** Drying speed and fermentation conditions change the internal structure of the seed. Dense, uniformly dried beans (typical of washed) extract predictably. Porous, unevenly dried beans (typical of naturals) extract faster and less evenly — requiring gentler pressure and better puck prep.

3. **Solubility profile.** Fermentation byproducts are highly soluble — they extract early and fast. A natural-processed coffee can taste over-extracted at parameters that would under-extract a washed coffee from the same farm.

---

## The Methods

### Washed (Wet Process)

**What happens:** Cherry skin and mucilage are mechanically removed within hours of picking. Beans ferment briefly in water tanks (12-36 hours) to loosen remaining mucilage, then are washed clean and dried on raised beds or patios.

**Flavor signature:** Clean, transparent, origin-forward. Acidity is bright and well-defined. Body is medium. You taste the terroir — altitude, soil, variety — without fermentation overlay.

**Why it extracts the way it does:** Removing the fruit early produces a dense, uniform bean with predictable solubility. There are fewer volatile fermentation compounds, so extraction is linear and forgiving.

**Espresso implications:**
- Most tolerant of standard 9-bar extraction
- Responds well to temperature adjustments for fine-tuning acidity
- Classic profiles (fixed pressure, lever decline) work reliably
- Grind sensitivity is moderate — small changes produce proportional results

**Common origins:** Colombia, Kenya, Ethiopia (Yirgacheffe washed lots), Guatemala, most Central American specialty

---

### Natural (Dry Process)

**What happens:** Whole cherries are dried intact on raised beds or patios for 2-4 weeks. The seed ferments inside the fruit the entire time, absorbing sugars, esters, and organic acids from the decomposing mucilage and skin.

**Flavor signature:** Heavy body, intense sweetness, fruit-forward (blueberry, strawberry, tropical fruit). Can have winey or fermented notes. Acidity is lower and rounder than washed. At its best: juicy and complex. At its worst: boozy, overripe, or "dirty."

**Why it extracts the way it does:** Extended fruit contact produces beans with:
- Higher sugar content (caramelizes easily — risk of bitterness at high temps)
- More volatile fermentation compounds (extract very quickly)
- Lower density and more irregular internal structure (less uniform extraction)

This means naturals extract faster and less evenly than washed coffees. Over-extraction of fermentation compounds creates harsh, boozy, or muddy flavors.

**Espresso implications:**
- **Lower pressure** (1-2 bar below what you'd use for a washed coffee of the same roast) — reduces extraction of harsh fermentation byproducts
- **Bloom profiles recommended** — even saturation compensates for uneven bean structure
- **Extend pre-infusion** — gentle wetting reduces channeling risk from porous pucks
- **Temperature: lower end for roast level** — limits caramelization of high sugar content
- **Puck prep is critical** — uneven density means channeling is more likely

**Common origins:** Ethiopia (Guji, Sidamo natural lots), Brazil (common for commodity and specialty), Yemen

---

### Honey Process

**What happens:** Cherry skin is removed, but some or all of the mucilage is left on the bean during drying. The amount of mucilage retained defines the sub-type:

| Sub-type | Mucilage retained | Drying time | Character |
|----------|-------------------|-------------|-----------|
| **White honey** | ~10-20% | Fastest | Closest to washed — clean, mild sweetness |
| **Yellow honey** | ~25-50% | Fast | Light sweetness, hint of fruit, clean finish |
| **Red honey** | ~50-75% | Moderate | Noticeable sweetness, some fruit, medium body |
| **Black honey** | ~80-100% | Slowest | Approaching natural — heavy sweetness, fruity, full body |

The more mucilage, the more fermentation, the more the bean behaves like a natural.

**Flavor signature:** A spectrum between washed and natural. Yellow/white honey: clean sweetness with light fruit. Black honey: heavy body, intense sweetness, stone fruit or tropical notes. The honey process is Costa Rica's signature innovation.

**Why it extracts the way it does:** Partial mucilage retention creates a bean that is denser than a natural but more soluble than a washed. The gradient from white to black mirrors the gradient from washed to natural in extraction behavior.

**Espresso implications:**
- **Yellow/white honey**: Treat like a washed coffee. Standard pressure, classic profiles
- **Red honey**: Slight pressure reduction (0.5-1 bar below washed). Bloom optional but beneficial
- **Black honey**: Treat closer to a natural. Lower pressure, bloom profile, extended pre-infusion
- **Temperature**: Stay mid-range for roast level. Honey coffees reward temperature stability

**Common origins:** Costa Rica (pioneer), El Salvador, Brazil, Panama

---

### Anaerobic Fermentation

**What happens:** Beans (washed or natural) are sealed in oxygen-free tanks or barrels for controlled fermentation, typically 24-96 hours but sometimes longer. The absence of oxygen favors specific yeast and bacterial strains that produce distinctive esters and organic acids not found in conventional processing. Temperature and pH are often monitored.

**Flavor signature:** Intensely aromatic — tropical fruit, candy, wine, sometimes cinnamon or bubblegum depending on conditions. Can be polarizing. The best anaerobic lots are extraordinarily complex; poorly controlled ones taste artificial or chemical.

**Why it extracts the way it does:** Anaerobic fermentation creates very high concentrations of volatile aromatic compounds. These extract almost immediately on contact with hot water. High pressure or temperature causes rapid over-extraction of these volatiles, producing harsh or chemical-tasting cups.

**Espresso implications:**
- **Lowest pressure in the matrix** — 6-8 bar for light roast, 6-7 bar for dark
- **Bloom profiles strongly recommended** — even saturation is essential
- **Lower temperature** — reduces volatile extraction speed
- **Extended pre-infusion at low flow** (2-2.5 ml/s) — wets the puck gently before extraction pressure
- **Coarser grind than expected** — slows extraction of hyper-soluble compounds
- **Puck prep is non-negotiable** — these coffees punish channeling severely

**Common origins:** Colombia (leading innovator), Ethiopia, Panama, Costa Rica, increasingly worldwide

---

### Carbonic Maceration

**What happens:** Borrowed from winemaking (Beaujolais technique). Whole cherries are placed in sealed tanks filled with CO2, displacing oxygen. The cherries ferment anaerobically from the inside out for 24-72 hours before being depulped and dried. Some producers follow with a secondary fermentation (washed or natural style).

**Flavor signature:** Similar to anaerobic but often with more fruit-forward, juicy, "vinous" character. Think red wine, berry jam, floral notes. The CO2 environment produces a different ester profile than liquid anaerobic fermentation.

**Why it extracts the way it does:** Same principles as anaerobic — high volatile concentration, fast extraction of fermentation compounds. The intracellular fermentation can produce even more volatile aromatics than standard anaerobic.

**Espresso implications:**
- **Same guidelines as anaerobic** — low pressure, bloom profiles, gentle pre-infusion
- If anything, lean even more conservative (lower end of pressure range) than anaerobic
- **Turbo shots** can work surprisingly well — coarse grind + low pressure highlights the juicy quality without over-extracting volatiles

**Common origins:** Colombia (Sasa Sestic popularized this after WBC win), Costa Rica, Kenya (experimental lots)

### Wet-Hulled (Giling Basah)

**What happens:** Unique to Indonesia (especially Sumatra). Cherries are depulped and briefly fermented, then partially dried to ~30-35% moisture. At this stage, the parchment (the papery shell around the green bean) is mechanically removed — much earlier than in any other method, where parchment stays on until the bean reaches 11-12% moisture. The exposed green beans are then dried the rest of the way.

Removing parchment early exposes the soft, swollen bean to air and heat, causing unique chemical changes and giving the beans their characteristic dark blue-green color.

**Flavor signature:** Earthy, herbal, spicy, heavy body, low acidity. Cedar, tobacco, dark chocolate, mushroom. Polarizing — some love the intensity, others find it "dirty." This is the classic Indonesian cup profile.

**Why it extracts the way it does:** Early parchment removal and high-moisture handling create beans that are:
- Low density (extract quickly)
- High in earthy/herbal compounds that can become muddy at high extraction
- Lower in bright acids (less temperature-dependent than washed)

**Espresso implications:**
- **Lower temperature** (88-91°C even for medium roasts) — keeps earthy notes in check
- **Moderate to low pressure** (7-8 bar typical) — prevents over-extraction of heavy body
- **Shorter ratios** (1:1.5 to 1:2) often work better — concentrates desirable earthy/spicy notes without amplifying muddy ones
- **Avoid very long pre-infusion** — these pucks are soft and degrade quickly
- Traditional fixed-pressure profiles often work better than complex pressure curves

**Common origins:** Sumatra (Mandheling, Lintong, Gayo), Sulawesi, Java, Flores (all Indonesian)

---

### Monsooned

**What happens:** A deliberate aging process unique to India (Monsooned Malabar). Dried green beans are stored in open-sided warehouses during the monsoon season (June-September). Exposure to humid monsoon winds causes the beans to absorb moisture, swell to nearly double their original size, and lose most of their acidity through chemical change. The process takes 12-16 weeks.

Originally accidental — green coffee shipped from India to Europe in wooden sailing vessels would arrive transformed by months of humid sea air. When steamships shortened the journey, producers recreated the effect on land.

**Flavor signature:** Near-zero acidity, very heavy body, earthy and malty sweetness, notes of tobacco, cedar, nuts, and baking spices. Smooth and mellow rather than complex. Often described as "comfort coffee."

**Why it extracts the way it does:** Monsooned beans are:
- Very low density (puffed and porous) — extract extremely quickly
- Almost no organic acids left — temperature barely changes the acidity profile
- High in malty/earthy compounds that become harsh when over-extracted

**Espresso implications:**
- **Lowest temperature range** (88-90°C) — there's nothing to gain from high temps
- **Low pressure** (7-8 bar) — these beans are fragile and porous
- **Short ratio** (1:1.5 to 1:2) — longer pulls amplify the earthy notes past pleasant
- **Quick pre-infusion** — pucks saturate fast due to porosity; lingering causes mush
- **Gentle profiles only** — these beans are not suited for turbo or high-pressure extraction
- Popular as an espresso-blend base for body and sweetness (Italian tradition)

**Common origins:** Malabar Coast, India (almost exclusively)

## Quick Reference: Processing → Extraction Behavior

| Processing | Density | Solubility | Extraction Speed | Channeling Risk | Key Adjustment |
|-----------|---------|-----------|-----------------|----------------|----------------|
| Washed | High | Moderate | Standard | Low | Standard parameters work |
| Natural | Low-Med | High | Fast | High | Lower pressure, bloom, extend PI |
| Honey (Yellow) | Med-High | Moderate | Standard | Low-Med | Slight pressure reduction |
| Honey (Black) | Med | High | Fast | Med-High | Treat like natural |
| Anaerobic | Varies | Very High | Very Fast | High | Lowest pressure, bloom required |
| Carbonic Mac. | Varies | Very High | Very Fast | High | Same as anaerobic |
| Wet-Hulled | Low | High | Fast | Med | Lower temp, shorter ratio |
| Monsooned | Very Low | High | Very Fast | Med | Lowest temp, low pressure, short ratio |

## Processing and the Pressure Matrix

The pressure matrix in [PRESSURE_GUIDE.md](gaggimate://knowledge/PRESSURE_GUIDE.md) adjusts bar by roast × processing. The underlying principle:

> **More fermentation = more soluble compounds = lower pressure needed.**

Washed coffees have the least fermentation and tolerate the highest pressure. Anaerobic and carbonic maceration coffees have the most fermentation and need the lowest pressure. Naturals and dark honeys fall in between.

Within a processing method, darker roasts are more soluble (cell structure broken down by heat), so they need even lower pressure. The matrix captures both gradients.

---

*Processing is the single biggest contributor to a coffee's extraction personality — more than origin, more than variety, arguably more than roast level. Understanding what was done to the cherry tells you how to treat the bean.*
